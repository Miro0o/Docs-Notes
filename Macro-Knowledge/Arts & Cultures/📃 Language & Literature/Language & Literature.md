#### Morphological Typology

**Morphological typology**Â is aÂ [way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linguistic_typology "Linguistic typology")Â of classifying the languages of the world that groups languages according to their commonÂ [morphological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphology_\(linguistics\) "Morphology (linguistics)")Â structures. - [Analytic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_language "Analytic language")Â languages contain very littleÂ [inflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflection "Inflection"), instead relying on features likeÂ [word order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word_order "Word order")Â and auxiliary words to convey meaning.
- [Synthetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthetic_language "Synthetic language")Â languages, ones that are not analytic, are divided into two categories:Â [agglutinative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agglutinative_language "Agglutinative language")Â andÂ [fusional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fusional_language "Fusional language")Â languages.
	- **Agglutinative languages** rely primarily on discrete particles ([prefixes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prefix "Prefix"),Â [suffixes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suffix "Suffix"), andÂ [infixes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infix "Infix")) for inflection.
		- A further subcategory of agglutinative languages areÂ **[polysynthetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polysynthetic_language "Polysynthetic language")Â languages**, which takeÂ [agglutination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agglutination "Agglutination")Â to a higher level by constructing entire sentences, includingÂ [nouns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noun "Noun"), as one word.

##### Analytic Languages (åˆ†æžè¯­)
> ðŸ”— https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphological_typology#Analytic_languages

Analytic languages show a **low ratio** ofÂ [morphemes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphemes "Morphemes")Â toÂ [words](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Words "Words"); in fact, the correspondence is nearly one-to-one. Sentences in analytic languages are composed of independent root morphemes. Grammatical relations between words are expressed by separate words where they might otherwise be expressed by affixes, which are present to a minimal degree in such languages. There is little to no morphological change in words: they tend to be uninflected. Grammatical categories are indicated by word order (for example, inversion of verb and subject for interrogative sentences) or by bringing in additional words (for example, a word for "some" or "many" instead of a pluralÂ [inflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflection "Inflection")Â like EnglishÂ _-s_). Individual words carry a general meaning (root concept); nuances are expressed by other words. Finally, in analytic languages context and syntax are more important thanÂ [morphology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphology_\(linguistics\) "Morphology (linguistics)").

==Analytic languages include some of the majorÂ [East Asian languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Asian_languages "East Asian languages"), such asÂ [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language "Chinese language"), andÂ [Vietnamese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnamese_language "Vietnamese language").== Note that theÂ [ideographic writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideographic_writing "Ideographic writing")Â systems of these languages play a strong role in regimenting linguistic continuity according to an analytic, or isolating, morphology (cf.Â [orthography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthography "Orthography")).

Additionally,Â [English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language "English language")Â is moderately analytic, and it andÂ [Afrikaans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afrikaans "Afrikaans")Â can be considered as some of the most analytic of all Indo-European languages. However, they are traditionally analyzed asÂ [fusional languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fusional_language "Fusional language").

> A related concept is theÂ **[isolating language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isolating_language "Isolating language")**, one in which there is only one, or on average close to one,Â [morpheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morpheme "Morpheme")Â per word. ##### Synthetic Languages (ç»¼åˆè¯­)
> ðŸ”— https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphological_typology#Synthetic_languages

Synthetic languages form words by **affixing a given number of dependent morphemes to a root morpheme**. The morphemes may be distinguishable from the root, or they may not. They may be fused with it or among themselves (in that multiple pieces of grammatical information may potentially be packed into one morpheme). Word order is less important for these languages than it is for analytic languages, since individual words express the grammatical relations that would otherwise be indicated by syntax. In addition, there tends to be a high degree ofÂ [concordance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agreement_\(linguistics\) "Agreement (linguistics)")Â (agreement, or cross-reference between different parts of the sentence). **Therefore, morphology in synthetic languages is more important than syntax.** MostÂ [Indo-European languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-European_languages "Indo-European languages")Â are moderately synthetic.
