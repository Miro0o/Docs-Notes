## Intro
> 🔗 https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_mathematics

The **history of mathematics** deals with the origin of discoveries in [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") and the [mathematical methods and notation of the past](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_mathematical_notation "History of mathematical notation"). Before the [modern age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_age "Modern age") and worldwide spread of knowledge, written examples of new mathematical developments have come to light only in a few locales. From 3000 BC the [Mesopotamian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesopotamia "Mesopotamia") states of [Sumer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumer "Sumer"), [Akkad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akkad_\(region\) "Akkad (region)") and [Assyria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assyria "Assyria"), followed closely by [Ancient Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt "Ancient Egypt") and the Levantine state of [Ebla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ebla "Ebla") began using [arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic "Arithmetic"), [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra "Algebra") and [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") for [taxation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxation "Taxation"), [commerce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commerce "Commerce"), trade, and in [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy"), to record time and formulate [calendars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calendars "Calendars").

The earliest mathematical texts available are from Mesopotamia and [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt "Ancient Egypt") – _[Plimpton 322](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plimpton_322 "Plimpton 322")_ ([Babylonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylonian_mathematics "Babylonian mathematics") c. 2000 – 1900 BC), the _[Rhind Mathematical Papyrus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhind_Mathematical_Papyrus "Rhind Mathematical Papyrus")_ ([Egyptian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_mathematics "Egyptian mathematics") c. 1800 BC) and the _[Moscow Mathematical Papyrus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moscow_Mathematical_Papyrus "Moscow Mathematical Papyrus")_ (Egyptian c. 1890 BC). All these texts mention the so-called [Pythagorean triples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_triple "Pythagorean triple"), so, by inference, the [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem") seems to be the most ancient and widespread mathematical development, after basic arithmetic and geometry.

The study of mathematics as a "demonstrative discipline" began in the 6th century BC with the [Pythagoreans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoreans "Pythagoreans"), who coined the term "mathematics" from the ancient [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language") _μάθημα_ (_mathema_), meaning "subject of instruction". [Greek mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_mathematics "Greek mathematics") greatly refined the methods (especially through the introduction of deductive reasoning and [mathematical rigor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_rigor "Mathematical rigor") in [proofs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_proof "Mathematical proof")) and expanded the subject matter of mathematics. The [ancient Romans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Romans "Ancient Romans") used [applied mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Applied_mathematics "Applied mathematics") in [surveying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surveying "Surveying"), [structural engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structural_engineering "Structural engineering"), [mechanical engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_engineering "Mechanical engineering"), [bookkeeping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bookkeeping "Bookkeeping"), creation of [lunar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_calendar "Lunar calendar") and [solar calendars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_calendar "Solar calendar"), and even [arts and crafts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_art "Roman art"). [Chinese mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_mathematics "Chinese mathematics") made early contributions, including a [place value system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Place_value_system "Place value system") and the first use of [negative numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_numbers "Negative numbers"). The [Hindu–Arabic numeral system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu%E2%80%93Arabic_numeral_system "Hindu–Arabic numeral system") and the rules for the use of its operations, in use throughout the world today, evolved over the course of the first millennium AD in [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_mathematics "Indian mathematics") and were transmitted to the [Western world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_world "Western world") via [Islamic mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_mathematics "Islamic mathematics") through the work of [Khwārizmī](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khw%C4%81rizm%C4%AB "Khwārizmī"). Islamic mathematics, in turn, developed and expanded the mathematics known to these civilizations. Contemporaneous with but independent of these traditions were the mathematics developed by the [Maya civilization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_civilization "Maya civilization") of [Mexico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mexico "Mexico") and [Central America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_America "Central America"), where the concept of [zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero "Zero") was given a standard symbol in [Maya numerals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_numerals "Maya numerals").

Many Greek and Arabic texts on mathematics were [translated into Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_translations_of_the_12th_century "Latin translations of the 12th century") from the 12th century, leading to further development of mathematics in [Medieval Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Ages "Middle Ages"). From ancient times through the [Middle Ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postclassical_age "Postclassical age"), periods of mathematical discovery were often followed by centuries of stagnation. Beginning in [Renaissance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renaissance "Renaissance") Italy in the 15th century, new mathematical developments, interacting with new scientific discoveries, were made at an [increasing pace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_growth "Exponential growth") that continues through the present day. This includes the groundbreaking work of both [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton") and [Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz "Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz") in the development of infinitesimal [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus") during the 17th century and following discoveries of [German mathematicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_German_mathematicians "List of German mathematicians") like [Carl Friedrich Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss "Carl Friedrich Gauss") and [David Hilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Hilbert "David Hilbert").



## The Development of Math
> 🔗 https://zh.wikipedia.org/zh-cn/%E6%95%B0%E5%AD%A6%E5%8F%B2#

数学史的主要研究对象是历史上的数学发现，调查它们的起源，或更广义地说，数学史就是对过去的数学方法与数学符号的探究。

**数学起源于人类早期的生产活动，为古中国六艺之一，亦被古希腊学者视为哲学之起点。** 数学最早用于人们计数、天文、度量甚至是贸易的需要。==这些需要可以简单地被概括为数学对结构、空间以及时间的研究；==对结构的研究是从数字开始的，首先是从我们称之为初等代数的——自然数和整数以及它们的算术关系式开始的。更深层次的研究是数论；对空间的研究则是从几何学开始的，首先是欧几里得几何和类似于三维空间[注 1]的三角学。后来产生了非欧几里得几何，在相对论中扮演着重要角色。

欧几里得所著《几何原本》中的一个证明 —— 被广泛认为是历史上最具影响力的教科书。[1]
在进入知识可以向全世界传播的现代社会以前，有记录的新数学发现仅仅在很少几个地区重见天日。目前最古老的数学文本是《普林顿 322》（古巴比伦，约公元前1900年[2]），《莱因德数学纸草书》（古埃及，约公元前2000年-1800年[3]），以及《莫斯科数学纸草书》（古埃及，约公元前1890年）。以上这些文本都涉及到了如今被称为毕达哥拉斯定理的概念，后者可能是继简单算术和几何后，最古老和最广泛传播的数学发现。

在公元前6世纪后，毕达哥拉斯将数学作为一门实证的学科进行研究，他创造了古希腊语单词μάθημα（mathema），意为“（被人们学习的）知识学问”[4]。希腊数学家在相当大的程度上改进了这些数学方法[注 2]，并扩大了数学的主题[5]。中国数学做了早期贡献，包括引入了位值制系统[6][7]。如今大行于世的印度-阿拉伯数字系统和运算方法，很可能是在公元后1000年的印度逐渐演化，并被伊斯兰数学家通过花拉子米的著作将其传到了西方[8][9]。伊斯兰数学则将以上这些文明的数学做了进一步的发展贡献。许多古希腊和伊斯兰数学著作随后被翻译成了拉丁文，引领了中世纪欧洲更深入的数学发展[10]。

从16世纪文艺复兴时期的意大利开始，算术、初等代数及三角学等初等数学已大体完备。17世纪变数概念的产生使人们开始研究变化中的量与量的互相关系和图形间的互相变换。随着自然科学和技术的进一步发展，为研究数学基础而产生的集合论和数理逻辑等也开始慢慢发展。

从古代到中世纪，数学发展的历史时期都伴随着数个世纪的停滞，但从16世纪以来，新的数学发展伴随新的科学发展，让数学不断加速大步前进，直至今日。

### Renaissance
> 🔗 https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_mathematics#Renaissance

During the [Renaissance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renaissance "Renaissance"), the development of mathematics and of [accounting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accounting "Accounting") were intertwined. While there is no direct relationship between algebra and accounting, the teaching of the subjects and the books published often intended for the children of merchants who were sent to reckoning schools (in [Flanders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flanders "Flanders") and [Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany "Germany")) or [abacus schools](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abacus_school "Abacus school") (known as _abbaco_ in Italy), where they learned the skills useful for trade and commerce. There is probably no need for algebra in performing [bookkeeping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bookkeeping "Bookkeeping") operations, but for complex bartering operations or the calculation of [compound interest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_interest "Compound interest"), a basic knowledge of arithmetic was mandatory and knowledge of algebra was very useful.

[Piero della Francesca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piero_della_Francesca "Piero della Francesca") (c. 1415–1492) wrote books on [solid geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry "Solid geometry") and [linear perspective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perspective_\(graphical\) "Perspective (graphical)"), including _[De Prospectiva Pingendi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Prospectiva_Pingendi "De Prospectiva Pingendi") (On Perspective for Painting)_, _Trattato d’Abaco (Abacus Treatise)_, and _[De quinque corporibus regularibus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_quinque_corporibus_regularibus "De quinque corporibus regularibus") (On the Five Regular Solids)_.

[Luca Pacioli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luca_Pacioli "Luca Pacioli")'s _[Summa de Arithmetica, Geometria, Proportioni et Proportionalità](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summa_de_arithmetica "Summa de arithmetica")_ (Italian: "Review of [Arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic "Arithmetic"), [Geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), [Ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ratio "Ratio") and [Proportion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportionality_\(mathematics\) "Proportionality (mathematics)")") was first printed and published in [Venice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venice "Venice") in 1494. It included a 27-page treatise on bookkeeping, _"Particularis de Computis et Scripturis"_ (Italian: "Details of Calculation and Recording"). It was written primarily for, and sold mainly to, merchants who used the book as a reference text, as a source of pleasure from the [mathematical puzzles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_puzzles "Mathematical puzzles") it contained, and to aid the education of their sons. In _Summa Arithmetica_, Pacioli introduced symbols for [plus and minus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plus_and_minus "Plus and minus") for the first time in a printed book, symbols that became standard notation in Italian Renaissance mathematics. _Summa Arithmetica_ was also the first known book printed in Italy to contain algebra. Pacioli obtained many of his ideas from Piero Della Francesca whom he plagiarized.

In Italy, during the first half of the 16th century, [Scipione del Ferro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scipione_del_Ferro "Scipione del Ferro") and [Niccolò Fontana Tartaglia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Fontana_Tartaglia "Niccolò Fontana Tartaglia") discovered solutions for [cubic equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubic_equation "Cubic equation"). [Gerolamo Cardano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gerolamo_Cardano "Gerolamo Cardano") published them in his 1545 book _[Ars Magna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ars_Magna_\(Gerolamo_Cardano\) "Ars Magna (Gerolamo Cardano)")_, together with a solution for the [quartic equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quartic_equation "Quartic equation"), discovered by his student [Lodovico Ferrari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lodovico_Ferrari "Lodovico Ferrari"). In 1572 [Rafael Bombelli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rafael_Bombelli "Rafael Bombelli") published his _L'Algebra_ in which he showed how to deal with the [imaginary quantities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number "Imaginary number") that could appear in Cardano's formula for solving cubic equations.

[Simon Stevin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simon_Stevin "Simon Stevin")'s _[De Thiende](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Thiende "De Thiende")_ ('the art of tenths'), first published in Dutch in 1585, contained the first systematic treatment of [decimal notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_notation "Decimal notation") in Europe, which influenced all later work on the [real number system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number_system "Real number system").

Driven by the demands of navigation and the growing need for accurate maps of large areas, [trigonometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometry "Trigonometry") grew to be a major branch of mathematics. [Bartholomaeus Pitiscus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bartholomaeus_Pitiscus "Bartholomaeus Pitiscus") was the first to use the word, publishing his _Trigonometria_ in 1595. Regiomontanus's table of sines and cosines was published in 1533.

During the Renaissance the desire of artists to represent the natural world realistically, together with the rediscovered philosophy of the Greeks, led artists to study mathematics. They were also the engineers and architects of that time, and so had need of mathematics in any case. The art of painting in perspective, and the developments in geometry that were involved, were studied intensely

### Modern  Mathematics 
#### 19th Century
> 🔗 https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_mathematics#19th_century

Throughout the 19th century mathematics became increasingly abstract. [Carl Friedrich Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss "Carl Friedrich Gauss") (1777–1855) did revolutionary work on [functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_\(mathematics\) "Function (mathematics)") of [complex variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_variable "Complex variable"), in [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), and on the convergence of [series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Series_\(mathematics\) "Series (mathematics)"), leaving aside his many contributions to science. He also gave the first satisfactory proofs of the [fundamental theorem of algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_algebra "Fundamental theorem of algebra") and [quadratic reciprocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_reciprocity "Quadratic reciprocity") law.

This century saw the development of the two forms of [non-Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Euclidean_geometry "Non-Euclidean geometry"), where the [parallel postulate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_postulate "Parallel postulate") of Euclidean geometry no longer holds. The Russian mathematician [Nikolai Ivanovich Lobachevsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nikolai_Ivanovich_Lobachevsky "Nikolai Ivanovich Lobachevsky") and his rival, the Hungarian mathematician [János Bolyai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C3%A1nos_Bolyai "János Bolyai"), independently defined and studied [hyperbolic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry "Hyperbolic geometry"), where uniqueness of parallels no longer holds. In this geometry the sum of angles in a triangle add up to less than 180°. [Elliptic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_geometry "Elliptic geometry") was developed later in the 19th century by the German mathematician [Bernhard Riemann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernhard_Riemann "Bernhard Riemann"); here no parallel can be found and the angles in a triangle add up to more than 180°. Riemann also developed [Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_geometry "Riemannian geometry"), which unifies and vastly generalizes the three types of geometry, and he defined the concept of a [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold"), which generalizes the ideas of [curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve") and [surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_\(topology\) "Surface (topology)"), and set the mathematical foundations for the [theory of general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity").

The 19th century saw the beginning of a great deal of [abstract algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_algebra "Abstract algebra"). [Hermann Grassmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Grassmann "Hermann Grassmann") in Germany gave a first version of [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space"), [William Rowan Hamilton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Rowan_Hamilton "William Rowan Hamilton") in Ireland developed [noncommutative algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative_algebra "Noncommutative algebra"). The British mathematician [George Boole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Boole "George Boole") devised an algebra that soon evolved into what is now called [Boolean algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boolean_algebra "Boolean algebra"), in which the only numbers were 0 and 1. Boolean algebra is the starting point of [mathematical logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_logic "Mathematical logic") and has important applications in [electrical engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_engineering "Electrical engineering") and [computer science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science "Computer science"). and [Karl Weierstrass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Weierstrass "Karl Weierstrass") reformulated the calculus in a more rigorous fashion.

Also, for the first time, the limits of mathematics were explored. [Paolo Ruffini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paolo_Ruffini "Paolo Ruffini"), [Niels Henrik Abel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niels_Henrik_Abel "Niels Henrik Abel"), and [Évariste Galois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89variste_Galois "Évariste Galois") proved there is no general algebraic method for solving polynomial equations of degree greater than four ([Abel–Ruffini theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abel%E2%80%93Ruffini_theorem "Abel–Ruffini theorem")). Other 19th-century mathematicians used this in their proofs that straight edge and compass alone are not sufficient to [trisect an arbitrary angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trisect_an_arbitrary_angle "Trisect an arbitrary angle"), to construct the side of a cube twice the volume of a given cube, [nor to construct a square equal in area to a given circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squaring_the_circle "Squaring the circle"). Mathematicians had vainly attempted to solve these problems since the ancient Greeks. On the other hand, the limitation of three [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension") in geometry was surpassed in the 19th century through considerations of [parameter space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter_space "Parameter space") and [hypercomplex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomplex_number "Hypercomplex number").

Abel and Galois's investigations into the solutions of various polynomial equations laid the groundwork for further developments of [group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_theory "Group theory"), and the associated fields of [abstract algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_algebra "Abstract algebra"). In the 20th century physicists and other scientists have seen group theory as the ideal way to study [symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry "Symmetry").

In the later 19th century, [Georg Cantor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Cantor "Georg Cantor") established the first foundations of [set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory "Set theory"), which enabled the rigorous treatment of the notion of infinity and has become the common language of nearly all mathematics. Cantor's set theory, and the rise of [mathematical logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_logic "Mathematical logic") in the hands of [Peano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peano "Peano"), [L.E.J. Brouwer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L.E.J._Brouwer "L.E.J. Brouwer"), [David Hilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Hilbert "David Hilbert"), [Bertrand Russell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bertrand_Russell "Bertrand Russell"), and [A.N. Whitehead](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A.N._Whitehead "A.N. Whitehead"), initiated a long running debate on the [foundations of mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foundations_of_mathematics "Foundations of mathematics").

The 19th century saw the founding of a number of national mathematical societies: the [London Mathematical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London_Mathematical_Society "London Mathematical Society") in 1865,) the [Société mathématique de France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soci%C3%A9t%C3%A9_math%C3%A9matique_de_France "Société mathématique de France") in 1872, the [Circolo Matematico di Palermo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circolo_Matematico_di_Palermo "Circolo Matematico di Palermo") in 1884, the [Edinburgh Mathematical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edinburgh_Mathematical_Society "Edinburgh Mathematical Society") in 1883,) and the [American Mathematical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Mathematical_Society "American Mathematical Society") in 1888. The first international, special-interest society, the [Quaternion Association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion_Association "Quaternion Association"), was formed in 1899, in the context of a [vector controversy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_quaternion#Historical_review "Hyperbolic quaternion"). In 1897, [Kurt Hensel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurt_Hensel "Kurt Hensel") introduced [p-adic numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-adic_number "P-adic number").
##### Foundational crisis of mathematics
> ↗ [Mathematical Logic (Foundations of Mathematics)](🤼‍♀️%20Mathematical%20Logic%20(Foundations%20of%20Mathematics)/Mathematical%20Logic%20(Foundations%20of%20Mathematics).md)

> 🔗 https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foundations_of_mathematics#Foundational_crisis

The **foundational crisis of mathematics** arose at the end of the 19th century and the beginning of the 20th century with the discovery of several [paradoxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paradox "Paradox") or counter-intuitive results.

The first one was the proof that the [parallel postulate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_postulate "Parallel postulate") cannot be proved. This results from a construction of a [non-Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Euclidean_geometry "Non-Euclidean geometry") inside [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"), whose [inconsistency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inconsistency "Inconsistency") would imply the inconsistency of Euclidean geometry. A well known paradox is [Russell's paradox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russell%27s_paradox "Russell's paradox"), which shows that the phrase "the set of all sets that do not contain themselves" is self-contradictory. Other philosophical problems were the proof of the existence of [mathematical objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_object "Mathematical object") that cannot be computed or explicitly described, and the proof of the existence of theorems on [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number") that cannot be proved with [Peano arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peano_arithmetic "Peano arithmetic") (see [Gödel's incompleteness theorems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6del%27s_incompleteness_theorems "Gödel's incompleteness theorems")).

Several schools of [philosophy of mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy_of_mathematics "Philosophy of mathematics") were challenged with these problems in the 20th century, and are described below.

==These problems were also studied by mathematicians, and this led to establish [mathematical logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_logic "Mathematical logic") as a new area of mathematics, consisting of providing mathematical definitions to logics (sets of [inference rules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inference_rules "Inference rules")), mathematical and logical theories, theorems, and proofs, and of using mathematical methods to prove theorems about these concepts.== The [Principia Mathematica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principia_Mathematica "Principia Mathematica") is a landmark result in mathematical logic and foundations published by Russell and [Alfred North Whitehead](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_North_Whitehead "Alfred North Whitehead") in 1913.

Mathematical logic led to unexpected results, such as [Gödel's incompleteness theorems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6del%27s_incompleteness_theorems "Gödel's incompleteness theorems"), which, roughly speaking, assert that, if a theory contains the standard arithmetic, it cannot be used to prove that it itself is not [self-contradictory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consistent_theory "Consistent theory"); and, if it is not self-contradictory, there are theorems that cannot be proved inside the theory, but are nevertheless true in some technical sense.

[Zermelo–Fraenkel set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zermelo%E2%80%93Fraenkel_set_theory "Zermelo–Fraenkel set theory") with the [axiom of choice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_choice "Axiom of choice") (ZFC) is a logical theory established by [Ernst Zermelo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernst_Zermelo "Ernst Zermelo") and [Abraham Fraenkel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abraham_Fraenkel "Abraham Fraenkel"). It became the standard foundation of modern mathematics, and, unless the contrary is explicitly specified, it is used in all modern mathematical texts, generally implicitly.

Simultaneously, the [axiomatic method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiomatic_method "Axiomatic method") became a de facto standard: the proof of a theorem must result from explicit [axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom "Axiom") and previously proved theorems by the application of clearly defined inference rules. The axioms need not correspond to some reality. Nevertheless, it is an open philosophical problem to explain why the axiom systems that lead to rich and useful theories are those resulting from abstraction from the physical reality or other mathematical theory.

In summary, the foundational crisis is essentially resolved, and this opens new philosophical problems. In particular, it cannot be proved that the new foundation (ZFC) is not self-contradictory. It is a general consensus that, if this would happen, the problem could be solved by a mild modification of ZFC.
#### 20th Century

#### 21st Century
> 🔗 https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_mathematics#21st_century

In 2000, the [Clay Mathematics Institute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clay_Mathematics_Institute "Clay Mathematics Institute") announced the seven [Millennium Prize Problems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millennium_Prize_Problems "Millennium Prize Problems"). In 2003 the [Poincaré conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_conjecture "Poincaré conjecture") was solved by [Grigori Perelman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grigori_Perelman "Grigori Perelman") (who declined to accept an award, as he was critical of the mathematics establishment).

Most mathematical journals now have online versions as well as print versions, and many online-only journals are launched. There is an increasing drive toward [open access publishing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_access_\(publishing\) "Open access (publishing)"), first made popular by [arXiv](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv "ArXiv").

Many other important problems have been solved in this century. Examples include the [Green–Tao theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green%E2%80%93Tao_theorem "Green–Tao theorem") (2004), [existence of bounded gaps between arbitrarily large primes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yitang_Zhang#Research "Yitang Zhang") (2013), and the [modularity theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modularity_theorem "Modularity theorem") (2001). The [AKS primality test](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AKS_primality_test "AKS primality test") was published in 2002, which is the first algorithm that can determine whether a number is prime or composite in [polynomial time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_complexity "Time complexity"). A proof of [Goldbach's weak conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goldbach%27s_weak_conjecture "Goldbach's weak conjecture") was published by [Harald Helfgott](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harald_Helfgott "Harald Helfgott") in 2013; as of 2025, the proof has not yet been fully reviewed. The first [einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_problem "Einstein problem") was discovered in 2023.

In addition, a lot of work has been done toward long-lasting projects which began in the twentieth century. For example, the [classification of finite simple groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classification_of_finite_simple_groups "Classification of finite simple groups") was completed in 2008. Similarly, work on the [Langlands program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Langlands_program "Langlands program") has progressed significantly, and there have been proofs of the [fundamental lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_lemma_\(Langlands_program\) "Fundamental lemma (Langlands program)") (2008), as well as a proposed proof of the [geometric Langlands correspondence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_Langlands_correspondence "Geometric Langlands correspondence") in 2024.
