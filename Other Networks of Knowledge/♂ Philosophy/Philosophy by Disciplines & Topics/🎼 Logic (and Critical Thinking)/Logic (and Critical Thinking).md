Logic studies **arguments**, which consist of a set of **premises** that leads to a **conclusion**. Arguments can be either correct or incorrect. **Deductive reasoning**Â is the process of drawing validÂ [inferences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inference "Inference"). An inference isÂ [valid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Validity_\(logic\) "Validity (logic)")Â if its conclusion followsÂ [logically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic "Logic")Â from itsÂ [premises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Premise "Premise"), meaning that it is impossible for the premises to be true and the conclusion to be false. For example, the inference from the premises "all men are mortal" and "[Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates "Socrates")Â is a man" to the conclusion "Socrates is mortal" is deductively valid. AnÂ [argument](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argument "Argument")Â isÂ _[sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soundness "Soundness")_Â if it is validÂ _and_Â all its premises are true. One approach defines deduction in terms of the intentions of the author: they have to intend for the premises to offer deductive support to the conclusion. With the help of this modification, it is possible to distinguish valid from invalid deductive reasoning: it is invalid if the author's belief about the deductive support is false, but even invalid deductive reasoning is a form of deductive reasoning.

**Deductive logic**Â studies under what conditions an argument is valid. According to theÂ [semantic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semantic "Semantic")Â approach, an argument is valid if there is no possibleÂ [interpretation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interpretation_\(logic\) "Interpretation (logic)")Â of the argument whereby its premises are true and its conclusion is false. TheÂ [syntactic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syntactic "Syntactic")Â approach, by contrast, focuses onÂ [rules of inference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rules_of_inference "Rules of inference"), that is, schemas of drawing a conclusion from a set of premises based only on theirÂ [logical form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_form "Logical form"). There are various rules of inference, such asÂ [modus ponens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modus_ponens "Modus ponens")Â andÂ [modus tollens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modus_tollens "Modus tollens"). Invalid deductive arguments, which do not follow a rule of inference, are calledÂ [formal fallacies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal_fallacies "Formal fallacies"). Rules of inference are definitory rules and contrast with strategic rules, which specify what inferences one needs to draw in order to arrive at an intended conclusion.
